%% ABSTRACT
%%=============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Discovering Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) with high CO$_2$ adsorption capacity
is essential for scalable carbon capture technology, yet systematic high-throughput
screening via molecular simulation remains prohibitively expensive for the
combinatorially vast design space.  We present a multi-modal deep learning
architecture that predicts the gravimetric CO$_2$ uptake at 1.0\,bar directly
from crystal structure with no simulation input at inference time.  Three
parallel information streams—a SchNet-based graph neural network (GNN) operating
on an 8\,\AA\ radius-cutoff atomic graph, a Magpie-inspired 145-dimensional
chemical composition encoder, and a quantum-chemistry descriptor branch with
learnable missing-data embeddings—are fused via a novel \emph{cross-attention}
module in which composition queries selectively attend to structural keys and
values across four parallel heads.  Training on 26{,}214 hypothetical MOF
structures with CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts scheduling and layer-wise learning
rates, the proposed architecture reaches a peak validation $R^2=0.8755$ and
test $R^2=0.8632$, $\text{MAE}=0.255$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$,
$\text{RMSE}=0.369$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$ on a held-out set of 3{,}277 structures.
A controlled structural ablation shows that cross-attention fusion strictly
outperforms concatenation-based mixing, and that each feature modality
contributes independently to predictive accuracy.  Transfer learning to
10{,}818 experimental CoRE-MOF structures via a 30-epoch frozen-GNN protocol
reduces the mean absolute error from 0.370 to 0.078\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$ with fewer
than 1{,}000 labelled target structures.  The full pipeline integrates seven
heterogeneous databases totalling approximately 118{,}000 structures, and the
complete implementation with all pre-processed data splits is publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Metal-Organic Frameworks, CO$_2$ adsorption, graph neural networks,

\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Anthropogenic CO$_2$ emissions represent the primary driver of global
temperature rise, and the 2015 Paris Agreement requires net-zero emissions
by mid-century across virtually all economic scenarios.
Post-combustion carbon capture, in which CO$_2$ is extracted from flue gas
before entering the atmosphere, is widely regarded as one of the few
near-term technologies capable of decarbonising heavy industry, cement
production, and fossil-fuelled power generation \cite{ramprasad2019}.
Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs) are a class of crystalline, nanoporous
coordination polymers formed by the self-assembly of inorganic metal nodes
and organic linker molecules.  Their exceptional surface areas—reaching
7{,}000\,m$^2$\,g$^{-1}$—tunable pore geometries, and rich chemical
diversity make them among the most promising solid sorbents for reversible
CO$_2$ storage at mild conditions \cite{jablonka2020,demir2022}.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{mof_crystal.png}
  \caption{Representative Metal-Organic Framework crystal showing the
           three-dimensional periodic porous lattice.  Metal-ion clusters
           (large nodes) are bridged by organic linkers (rods), creating
           cavities of controlled size in which CO$_2$ molecules (linear
           triatomic, highlighted) adsorb.  The diversity of possible node
           chemistry, linker length, and topology gives rise to the
           astronomically large MOF design space that motivates machine-learning
           screening.}
  \label{fig:mof_crystal}
\end{figure}

The combinatorial scope of achievable MOF topologies has been estimated to
exceed $10^{60}$ distinct structures \cite{wilmer2012}.  Even restricting
attention to hypothetical structures generated by systematic combinatorial
enumeration (as in the hMOF database with 32{,}768 entries) renders brute-force
Grand Canonical Monte Carlo (GCMC) simulation—the computational gold standard
for adsorption prediction—impractical: at 10–100 CPU-hours per structure,
exhaustive screening of that library alone would demand more than
$3\times10^6$ CPU-hours \cite{jablonka2020}.

Machine learning surrogates trained on a representative GCMC-computed subset
can reduce inference to milliseconds per structure.  Early approaches used
hand-crafted geometric descriptors (void fraction, pore limiting diameter,
surface area) as inputs to gradient-boosted models, achieving $R^2 \approx 0.93$
on the same feature set \cite{cgcnn2018}.  Graph neural networks (GNNs) operate
directly on the atomic graph without manual feature engineering, learning
structure-property relationships end-to-end \cite{schnet2018,cgcnn2018,
megnet2019}.  However, pure graph approaches discard the rich compositional
chemistry encoded in elemental properties and any available quantum-chemical
data.  Conversely, composition-only models are blind to the pore topology
that governs adsorption capacity.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We propose a three-branch hybrid architecture that jointly learns
        from (i) a radius-cutoff crystal graph processed by SchNet-style
        message passing with global attention pooling, (ii) a 145-dimensional
        Magpie composition embedding processed by a deep MLP, and (iii)
        quantum-chemistry descriptors with a learnable missing-data embedding.
  \item We introduce a \textbf{cross-attention fusion module} in which
        composition-branch queries attend over graph-branch keys and values
        using scaled multi-head dot-product attention, enabling dynamic,
        context-sensitive weighting of structural information.
  \item We construct and analyse crystal graphs for 32{,}768 hypothetical
        MOF structures using a physics-motivated 8\,\AA\ radius cutoff that
        captures pore-scale neighbour topology beyond covalent bonds.
  \item We demonstrate effective cross-database transfer from hypothetical to
        experimental MOFs, achieving large MAE reductions with frozen structural
        representations and fewer than 1{,}000 labelled target structures.
  \item We provide a comprehensive ablation over five model configurations
        and three training strategies, with all metrics derived from
        held-out test sets rather than validation performance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Machine Learning for Porous Materials}

Jablonka~et~al.\ \cite{jablonka2020} conducted the first large-scale
bibliometric survey of ML applied to porous materials, identifying adsorption
prediction as the dominant use case and gradient-boosted trees as the most
successful algorithm class when geometric descriptors are available.
Moosavi~et~al.\ \cite{moosavi2020} analysed the chemical diversity of the
known MOF landscape and demonstrated that training-set bias toward certain
metal species severely limits out-of-distribution generalisation, motivating
the multi-database integration strategy adopted in this work.
Demir~et~al.\ \cite{demir2022} provided a comprehensive review of recent
ML approaches to CO$_2$ capture, distinguishing structure-based, composition-based,
and hybrid modelling strategies and noting that purely graph-based models
have not yet decisively outperformed tabular baselines on all benchmark sets.

\subsection{Crystal Graph Neural Networks}

Xie and Grossman \cite{cgcnn2018} introduced the Crystal Graph Convolutional
Neural Network (CGCNN), encoding atoms as graph nodes and bonds as multi-hot
edge feature vectors.  CGCNN demonstrated that gradient-computed representations
of crystal topology can match hand-crafted features for formation energy
prediction.  Schütt~et~al.\ \cite{schnet2018} proposed SchNet, a continuous-filter
convolutional network that expands scalar interatomic distances through a
learnable Gaussian radial basis and avoids the discrete bond-type vocabulary
of CGCNN, giving more physically principled edge representations.
Chen~et~al.\ \cite{megnet2019} extended the graph formalism to MEGNet by
incorporating global state attributes (temperature, pressure) alongside
per-atom and per-bond features.   Peng~et~al.\ \cite{peng2022} specifically
evaluated geometric deep learning for MOF adsorption prediction, finding that
incorporating pore-geometry information beyond covalent bonds is critical.
Fung~et~al.\ \cite{fung2021} performed a systematic benchmark of GNN depth,
width, pooling strategy, and edge encoding across multiple materials tasks,
confirming that attention-based pooling and distance-continuous edge features
consistently outperform simpler alternatives.

\subsection{Multi-Modal and Hybrid Architectures}

The fusion of graph-based and composition-based features has been explored in
the broader molecular ML literature.
Hu~et~al.\ \cite{hu2020ogb} benchmarked a wide range of graph encoding
strategies on the Open Graph Benchmark and showed that incorporating domain
features alongside learned representations reliably improves performance.
Batatia~et~al.\ \cite{mace2022} demonstrated that higher-order equivariant
message passing recovers accuracy approaching density functional theory for
interatomic force fields, at the cost of substantially increased computational
overhead compared to the distance-only SchNet formulation employed here.
Chen and Ong \cite{chen2022universal} showed that universal graph potentials
trained across the periodic table benefit from explicit elemental embeddings
alongside graph structure, aligning with our design decision to use a separate
chemical composition branch.

\subsection{Transfer Learning Across MOF Databases}

Witman~et~al.\ \cite{witman2020} showed that a model trained on hypothetical
MOFs retains predictive power after fine-tuning on a small experimental
dataset, establishing the feasibility of the hypothetical-to-experimental
transfer paradigm studied here.  The critical question of how much adaptation
data is needed—and whether to unfreeze the structural encoder—has not been
systematically studied on the large CoRE-MOF 2019 test set that we use
\cite{coremof2019}.

\subsection{Attention for Modality Fusion}

The scaled dot-product self-attention of Vaswani~et~al.\ \cite{attention2017}
is the foundation of the cross-attention module introduced here.  Unlike
self-attention operating within a single modality, cross-attention allows one
feature stream to act as query while another provides keys and values,
naturally implementing the intuition that compositional chemistry selectively
weights which structural features are relevant for prediction.

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}

\paragraph{Outlier Clipping}
Extreme-value outliers are removed per feature column using the
$1.5\times$IQR rule: values outside
$[\text{Q1}-1.5\,\text{IQR},\;\text{Q3}+1.5\,\text{IQR}]$
are clipped to the boundary.  For the primary target CO$_2$ @ 1.0\,bar
this clips 224 structures (0.68\%) to the interval
$[-1.89,\;7.57]$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$.  More aggressive clipping is applied
at lower pressures where the right tail is heavier (e.g., 11.81\% at
0.01\,bar).

\paragraph{Feature Normalisation}
A z-score standardisation (zero mean, unit variance) is fitted
\emph{exclusively} on the training partition and applied to validation and
test sets to prevent any form of leakage.

\paragraph{Stratified Splits}
Structures are binned into ten equal-width CO$_2$-uptake intervals and
sampled proportionally to ensure that the target distribution is preserved
across all three partitions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} = 26{,}214~(80\%),\quad
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}   = 3{,}277~(10\%),\quad
  \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}  = 3{,}277~(10\%).
  \label{eq:splits}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{COF Exclusion}
A manifest of 1{,}242 Covalent Organic Framework identifiers is compiled
from the CoRE-COFs v7.0 and CURATED-COFs catalogues and used to remove
non-metal-stranded structures from all MOF-specific modelling tasks.

\section{Problem Formulation and Feature Engineering}
\label{sec:features}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

Let $\mathcal{M} = \{(S_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$ be a labelled dataset where
$S_i$ is the crystal structure (CIF file) of the $i$-th MOF and
$y_i \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is its GCMC-computed CO$_2$ uptake.  We seek
a mapping $f_\theta: S \mapsto \hat{y}$ parameterised by $\theta$ such that
$\hat{y} \approx y$ on unseen structures.  Rather than computing $f_\theta$
from a single representation of $S$, we decompose $S$ into three complementary
views:
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{G}(S) &\;\triangleq\; \text{atomic radius graph},
                  \label{eq:G}\\
  \mathbf{c}(S)  &\;\triangleq\; \text{Magpie composition vector},
                  \label{eq:c}\\
  \mathbf{q}(S)  &\;\triangleq\; \text{DFT quantum-chemistry descriptor},
                  \label{eq:q_def}
\end{align}
and learn modality-specific encoders $g_\theta$, $h_\theta$, $k_\theta$,
followed by a cross-attention fusion $\mathcal{F}_\theta$ and a scalar
regression head $\psi_\theta$.

\subsection{Crystal Graph Construction}
\label{sec:graph_const}

Given a CIF text block containing the unit-cell parameters
$(a,b,c,\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$ and fractional atomic coordinates
$\{\mathbf{f}_i\}_{i=1}^{N_a}$, we first convert fractional coordinates
to Cartesian using the transformation matrix:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{M} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    a & b\cos\gamma & c\cos\beta \\
    0 & b\sin\gamma & c\,\frac{\cos\alpha-\cos\beta\cos\gamma}{\sin\gamma}\\
    0 & 0           & c\,\frac{\Omega}{\sin\gamma}
  \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:M}
\end{equation}
where
$\Omega = \sqrt{1-\cos^2\!\alpha-\cos^2\!\beta-\cos^2\!\gamma
                +2\cos\alpha\cos\beta\cos\gamma}$
is the unit-cell volume factor.
Cartesian positions satisfy $\mathbf{x}_i = \mathbf{M}\mathbf{f}_i$.

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{graph_construction.png}
  \caption{Crystal graph construction pipeline.  \textbf{Left}: raw CIF
           unit cell with atoms colour-coded by element.  \textbf{Centre}:
           graph abstraction where each atom becomes a labelled node and
           edges connect all pairs within the 8\,\AA\ radius cutoff (dashed
           circle), capturing pore-topology neighbours well beyond covalent
           bonds.  \textbf{Right}: the scalar pairwise distance is expanded
           into a 50-bin Gaussian RBF vector that serves as the continuous
           edge feature.}
  \label{fig:graph_const}
\end{figure}

The graph $\mathcal{G}=(V,E)$ is then defined by:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Nodes}: each atom is a node $v_i$ with feature vector
    \begin{equation}
      \mathbf{x}_i = \bigl[Z_i,\;\chi_i,\;r^{\text{cov}}_i\bigr]
                     \in \mathbb{R}^3,
      \label{eq:xnode}
    \end{equation}
    where $Z_i$ is the atomic number, $\chi_i$ the Pauling
    electronegativity, and $r^{\text{cov}}_i$ the covalent radius in pm.
    These three descriptors jointly encode nuclear charge (element identity),
    electron-attracting power (reactivity), and effective atomic size,
    covering the most fundamental atomic-scale determinants of host-guest
    interaction.
  \item \textbf{Edges}: $(i,j)\in E$ iff $0.1\Ang < d_{ij} < r_c$, where
    $r_c = 8.0$\,\AA\ and the 0.1\,\AA\ lower bound excludes
    numerically degenerate self-pairs.  Edges are undirected.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Choice of Cutoff Radius}
The conventional GNN literature for small molecules uses covalent-bond
cutoffs of $\sim$1.5\,\AA, capturing only bonded pairs.  For MOFs, the
CO$_2$ adsorption mechanism is governed by interactions with pore walls at
distances of 3--8\,\AA, far beyond any covalent bond.  An 8\,\AA\ cutoff
ensures that at least the first and second coordination shells of every
atom within the pore environment are represented in the graph, including
the metal node–organic linker distances that determine pore-limiting
diameter and surface potential.  Structures with more than 700 atoms per
unit cell are skipped to bound GPU memory.

\subsection{Gaussian Radial Basis Function Edge Encoding}
\label{sec:rbf}

To represent continuous interatomic distances in a fixed-dimensional vector
suitable for neural network processing, we apply a Gaussian RBF expansion:
\begin{equation}
  \phi_k(d) = \exp\!\bigl(-\gamma(d - \mu_k)^2\bigr),
  \quad k=1,\ldots,K,
  \label{eq:rbf}
\end{equation}
where $\{\mu_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$ are $K=50$ uniformly spaced centres in
$[0,\;6]$\,\AA, and $\gamma=10$\,\AA$^{-2}$ controls the bandwidth.
The resulting $\boldsymbol{\phi}(d)\in\mathbb{R}^{50}$ is the edge feature
vector.  The RBF expansion has three principal advantages over
distance-binning: (i) smooth interpolation between bins avoids
discontinuities at bin edges that could confuse the filter network;
(ii) the $K=50$ width provides sufficient resolution across the $[0,8]$\,\AA\
range while remaining computationally tractable; and (iii) the Gaussian
shape naturally models the Lennard-Jones-like short-range repulsion and
medium-range attraction relevant to physisorption.

\subsection{Magpie Chemical Composition Features}
\label{sec:magpie}

Let $\mathcal{Z}_m = \{e_1,\ldots,e_{n_m}\}$ be the set of unique elements
in MOF $m$.  For each element $e$, a property vector
$\mathbf{p}(e)\in\mathbb{R}^{29}$ is assembled from the Magpie elemental
property table \cite{cgcnn2018}, comprising: atomic number, atomic mass,
Pauling electronegativity, first ionisation energy, electron affinity,
covalent radius, van der Waals radius, boiling point, melting point,
density, molar volume, thermal conductivity, periodic-table group, period,
s/p/d/f valence electrons, s/p/d/f unfilled sub-shells, and six Boolean
flags (metal, transition metal, alkali, alkaline earth, halogen, lanthanide).
These properties encode the quantum-mechanical character of each element
across multiple complementary chemical dimensions.

The composition feature is constructed by computing five statistical
summaries over the element set:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}_{m}
  =\Bigl[
     \bar{\mathbf{p}}\,\|\,
     \sigma(\mathbf{p})\,\|\,
     \mathbf{p}_{\min}\,\|\,
     \mathbf{p}_{\max}\,\|\,
     (\mathbf{p}_{\max}-\mathbf{p}_{\min})
   \Bigr]
  \in\mathbb{R}^{145},
  \label{eq:magpie}
\end{equation}
where $\|$ denotes concatenation and $\bar{\mathbf{p}}, \sigma(\mathbf{p}),
\mathbf{p}_{\min}, \mathbf{p}_{\max}$ are respectively the element-wise
mean, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum across $\mathcal{Z}_m$
(5 statistics $\times$ 29 properties = 145 features).
The range statistic $\mathbf{p}_{\max}-\mathbf{p}_{\min}$ captures
elemental diversity: a MOF containing both an electropositive alkali metal
and an electronegative halogen will have a high electronegativity range,
which is informative for CO$_2$ binding via Lewis acid-base interactions.

\subsection{QMOF Quantum-Chemistry Descriptors}
\label{sec:qmof}

The QMOF database \cite{qmof2021} provides DFT-computed electronic structure
properties for approximately 20{,}000 MOF structures, including band gap,
metal partial charges, DDEC6 charge magnitudes, and magnetic moments.  These
electronic descriptors encode quantum-mechanical information not derivable
from the crystal geometry alone—in particular, the partial charges on metal
nodes directly modulate the CO$_2$ quadrupole interaction and hence
physisorption enthalpy.

We compile an 8-dimensional quantum descriptor vector $\mathbf{q}_m$ for
each MOF where QMOF overlap is found by structural name matching.
Approximately 60\% of hMOF structures have QMOF matches; for the remaining
40\%, imputing zeros would introduce a systematic bias in the learned
representation.  We instead adopt a \emph{learnable missing-data embedding}
(see Section~\ref{sec:arch_q}), ensuring that the model distinguishes between
a true zero quantum descriptor and a missing observation.

\section{Proposed Multi-Modal Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

\begin{figure}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{arch_diagram.png}
  \caption{End-to-end architecture of the proposed hybrid model.  Three
           parallel branches process the crystal graph (left, blue),
           chemical composition (centre, orange), and quantum descriptors
           (right, green).  A cross-attention fusion block (purple) enables
           composition queries to selectively attend over structural
           keys/values.  The fused embedding is concatenated with the
           quantum embedding and decoded by a MLP prediction head (red)
           to a scalar CO$_2$ uptake estimate.}
  \label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

The overall architecture, illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}, processes
each MOF through three specialised encoder branches before fusing their
outputs via cross-attention and producing a scalar regression target.

\subsection{Structural GNN Branch}
\label{sec:arch_gnn}

The GNN branch implements SchNet-style continuous-filter convolutional
message passing \cite{schnet2018}.  An initial linear embedding maps the
3-dimensional node feature $\mathbf{x}_i$ to a $d_h=128$ dimensional
hidden representation:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_i^{(0)} = \mathrm{SiLU}\!\bigl(\mathbf{W}_{\text{emb}}\mathbf{x}_i\bigr).
  \label{eq:h0}
\end{equation}
For each of $L=3$ interaction layers, an interaction block computes:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell+1)} = \mathbf{h}_i^{(\ell)}
    + \mathrm{SiLU}\!\Bigl(
        \mathbf{W}_\ell
        \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)}
        \mathbf{h}_j^{(\ell)} \odot
        \underbrace{
          \mathbf{W}_{\phi,\ell}^{(2)}\,
          \mathrm{SiLU}\!\bigl(
            \mathbf{W}_{\phi,\ell}^{(1)}\boldsymbol{\phi}(d_{ij})
          \bigr)
        }_{\text{continuous filter } W(d_{ij})}
      \Bigr),
  \label{eq:schnet}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\phi}(d_{ij})\in\mathbb{R}^{50}$ is the Gaussian RBF
expansion (Eq.~\ref{eq:rbf}), $\mathbf{W}_{\phi,\ell}^{(1)}\in\mathbb{R}^{128\times50}$
and $\mathbf{W}_{\phi,\ell}^{(2)}\in\mathbb{R}^{128\times128}$ form a
two-layer filter network, and $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication.
The continuous filter $W(d_{ij})\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$ plays the role of an
adaptive, distance-modulated message gate: large filter values amplify the
contribution of neighbouring atom $j$, and the Gaussian RBF inputs allow
the network to learn distinct gating profiles for chemically distinct
distance scales (covalent bonds, van der Waals contacts, pore-wall
interactions).  SiLU (Sigmoid-weighted Linear Unit) is used as the
non-linearity throughout the GNN because its smooth, non-monotone shape
avoids dying-ReLU failures and has been shown to improve convergence
depth in atom-level models \cite{mace2022}.

\paragraph{Global Attention Pooling}
After $L$ interaction layers, the final node embeddings
$\{\mathbf{h}_i^{(L)}\}$ are aggregated into a single graph-level vector
via attention pooling \cite{fung2021}.  A two-layer gate network
$g: \mathbb{R}^{128}\to\mathbb{R}$ assigns a scalar importance score to
each atom:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\alpha}_i = g\bigl(\mathbf{h}_i^{(L)}\bigr)
                 = \mathbf{w}_2^\top\,\mathrm{SiLU}(
                     \mathbf{W}_1\mathbf{h}_i^{(L)}+\mathbf{b}_1)+b_2.
  \label{eq:gate}
\end{equation}
Scores are normalised within each graph using numerically stable
max-subtracted softmax:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_i = \frac{\exp(\hat{\alpha}_i - \max_j\hat{\alpha}_j)}
                  {\sum_{k\in V_m}\exp(\hat{\alpha}_k - \max_j\hat{\alpha}_j)},
  \label{eq:attn_pool}
\end{equation}
and the graph representation is computed as:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{h}_{\mathcal{G}}
    = \sum_{i\in V_m}\alpha_i\,\mathbf{h}_i^{(L)}
    \in\mathbb{R}^{128}.
  \label{eq:pool}
\end{equation}
Attention pooling is strictly superior to mean pooling for MOFs because
adsorption is dominated by a small subset of high-energy binding sites
(typically near the open metal coordination sites), which the gate network
can learn to upweight.  A final linear projection with LayerNorm and GELU
activation produces the GNN branch output
$\mathbf{e}^{\text{gnn}}\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$.

\subsection{Chemical Composition Encoder Branch}
\label{sec:arch_chem}

The 145-dimensional Magpie vector $\mathbf{c}_m$ is processed by a
two-layer MLP with LayerNorm and dropout:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}^{\text{chem}}
  = \mathrm{GELU}\!\bigl(\mathrm{LN}(
      \mathbf{W}_2\,
      \mathrm{DO}_{0.15}(
        \mathrm{GELU}(
          \mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{W}_1\mathbf{c}_m)
        )
      )
    )\bigr)
  \in\mathbb{R}^{128},
  \label{eq:chem_enc}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W}_1\in\mathbb{R}^{256\times145}$,
$\mathbf{W}_2\in\mathbb{R}^{128\times256}$,
LN denotes LayerNorm, and DO$_p$ denotes Dropout with rate $p$.
LayerNorm is placed \emph{before} the non-linearity (Pre-LN) following
established practice for stable training of deep MLPs, and a dropout rate
of 0.15 on the first layer serves as a compositional feature mask that
prevents co-adaptation between correlated Magpie properties (e.g.,
covalent radius and atomic mass are highly correlated and could be
jointly memorised without dropout).  GELU is used rather than ReLU to
avoid hard-zeroing semantics in a signed composition feature space.

\subsection{Quantum Descriptor Branch with Missing-Data Handling}
\label{sec:arch_q}

The 8-dimensional DFT descriptor vector $\mathbf{q}_m$ is processed by a
two-layer MLP with GELU activations.  To handle the $\approx$40\% of
training samples with no QMOF match, we introduce a trainable
\emph{missing-data embedding} $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{miss}}\in\mathbb{R}^{64}$
initialised to $\mathbf{0}$ and jointly optimised with all other parameters:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}^{\text{q}}_m =
  \begin{cases}
    \mathrm{MLP}_q(\mathbf{q}_m)     & m\in\mathcal{Q}_{\text{avail}}, \\
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{miss}} & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:qbranch}
\end{equation}
This approach is superior to zero-imputation because (i) the model learns a
distinct code for ``quantum data absent'', which can be allocated a separate
region in feature space; (ii) gradients flow through
$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{miss}}$ during training on the missing subset,
allowing the missing-data embedding to specialise to the average behaviour
of that subset; and (iii) there is no distributional mismatch at test time
for structures without DFT data, unlike zero-imputation which would produce
an out-of-distribution input for a model trained on real zero-valued quantum
descriptors.

\subsection{Cross-Attention Fusion Module}
\label{sec:arch_fusion}

The core architectural innovation is the cross-attention module that fuses
the graph and composition streams.  Unlike self-attention, which attends
within a single feature sequence, cross-attention allows one representation
to act as query while another provides keys and values, implementing the
physically motivated intuition that \emph{what structural evidence to surface
depends on the chemical context}.

Given the GNN embedding $\mathbf{e}^{\text{gnn}}\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$ and
the chemical embedding $\mathbf{e}^{\text{chem}}\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$, the
module computes query, key, and value projections:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{Q} &= \mathbf{e}^{\text{chem}}\mathbf{W}^Q
               \in\mathbb{R}^{d_h},
  \label{eq:Qproj}\\
  \mathbf{K} &= \mathbf{e}^{\text{gnn}}\mathbf{W}^K
               \in\mathbb{R}^{d_h},
  \label{eq:Kproj}\\
  \mathbf{V} &= \mathbf{e}^{\text{gnn}}\mathbf{W}^V
               \in\mathbb{R}^{d_h},
  \label{eq:Vproj}
\end{align}
with $\mathbf{W}^Q,\mathbf{W}^K,\mathbf{W}^V\in\mathbb{R}^{128\times128}$.
The attention is computed with $H=4$ heads of dimension $d_k=d_h/H=32$:
\begin{equation}
  \text{head}_h
  = \softmax\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}_h\mathbf{K}_h^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)
    \mathbf{V}_h,
  \quad h=1,\ldots,H,
  \label{eq:head_h}
\end{equation}
and the multi-head output is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{e}^{\text{fused}}
  = \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl(
      [\text{head}_1\|\cdots\|\text{head}_H]\,\mathbf{W}^O
      + \mathbf{e}^{\text{chem}}
    \bigr)
  \in\mathbb{R}^{128}.
  \label{eq:cross_attn}
\end{equation}
The residual connection $+\,\mathbf{e}^{\text{chem}}$ stabilises early-phase
training when the attention weights are near-uniform and provides a direct
gradient path from the prediction loss to the composition branch, preventing
its collapse.  The $1/\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling prevents very small gradients
through the softmax when $d_k$ is large.

The directional asymmetry—composition queries, structural keys/values—is
physically motivated.  The composition determines \emph{what} to look for
(e.g., a Zn-carboxylate MOF should attend to Zn-O distances relevant to
paddle-wheel geometry), while the graph provides a complete catalogue of
observed distances.

\subsection{Prediction Head}
\label{sec:arch_head}

The fused embedding $\mathbf{e}^{\text{fused}}\in\mathbb{R}^{128}$ and the
quantum embedding $\mathbf{e}^{\text{q}}\in\mathbb{R}^{64}$ are
concatenated and decoded through:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y} = \mathbf{W}_3\,\mathrm{GELU}\!\bigl(
              \mathbf{W}_2\,\mathrm{GELU}(
                \mathrm{LN}(
                  \mathrm{DO}_{0.10}(
                    \mathbf{W}_1[\mathbf{e}^{\text{fused}}\|\mathbf{e}^{\text{q}}]
                  )
                )
              )
            \bigr),
  \label{eq:head1}
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{W}_1\in\mathbb{R}^{256\times192}$,
$\mathbf{W}_2\in\mathbb{R}^{128\times256}$,
$\mathbf{W}_3\in\mathbb{R}^{1\times128}$.
The concatenation of fused and quantum embeddings is deliberate: the quantum
branch provides information that may not be present in the crystal-graph or
Magpie representation (partial charges, magnetic moments), and its late
inclusion in the prediction head avoids confounding the cross-attention
fusion, which is designed to integrate graph and composition information.

\section{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}

\begin{algorithm}[!t]
\caption{Hybrid Model Training}
\label{alg:train}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Training set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ (26{,}214 structures),
         validation set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}$ (3{,}277),
         batch size $B=64$, total epochs $T=150$
\STATE Initialise all parameters $\theta$ (Xavier/He init)
\STATE Set \textbf{AdamW} optimiser with \emph{per-group} learning rates:
       $\eta_{\text{gnn}}=10^{-4}$ for GNN branch;
       $\eta_{\text{tab}}=10^{-3}$ for composition, quantum, fusion,
       and head;
       weight decay $\lambda=10^{-4}$ globally
\STATE Attach \textbf{CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts}
       scheduler: $T_0 = 50\cdot\lceil|\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}|/B\rceil$,
       $T_{\text{mult}} = 2$
\STATE Initialise best validation $R^2_* \leftarrow -\infty$
\FOR{epoch $t = 1$ \TO $T$}
  \FOR{each mini-batch $\mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$}
    \STATE $\hat{\mathbf{y}} \leftarrow f_\theta(\mathcal{B})$
    \STATE $\mathcal{L} \leftarrow \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}
           \sum \mathcal{L}_\delta(\hat{y}_i, y_i)$
           \hfill\COMMENT{Huber, $\delta=0.1$}
    \STATE $\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$.backward()
    \STATE \textbf{Clip} $\|\nabla_\theta\|_2 \leq 10.0$
    \STATE Optimiser step; scheduler step
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE $R^2_{\text{val}} \leftarrow \text{evaluate}(f_\theta,
         \mathcal{D}_{\text{val}})$
  \IF{$R^2_{\text{val}} > R^2_*$}
    \STATE $R^2_* \leftarrow R^2_{\text{val}}$;\ \
           save checkpoint $\theta^*$
  \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\theta^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Huber Loss}

The regression objective is the Huber loss with $\delta=0.1$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\delta(y,\hat{y}) =
  \begin{cases}
    \tfrac{1}{2}(y-\hat{y})^2
      & |y-\hat{y}| \leq \delta,\\
    \delta\!\left(|y-\hat{y}|-\tfrac{\delta}{2}\right)
      & |y-\hat{y}| > \delta.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:huber}
\end{equation}
The small value $\delta=0.1$ was chosen so that the quadratic regime
covers approximately one standard deviation of the residual distribution
for a well-trained model, providing MSE-like behaviour for typical
prediction errors while switching to MAE-like linear penalty for the
large residuals concentrated in the right tail of the uptake distribution.
This significantly reduces the influence of the 0.68\% of target values
that lie in the outlier region before IQR clipping is applied.

\subsection{Layer-wise Learning Rates}

Empirically, the GNN branch converges more slowly than the tabular branches
because message-passing gradients must propagate through $L=3$ interaction
layers and the filter networks, while the composition MLP receives direct
gradient signal from the prediction head.  Assigning the GNN branch a 10$\times$
lower learning rate ($\eta_{\text{gnn}}=10^{-4}$ vs.\ $\eta_{\text{tab}}=10^{-3}$)
prevents the tabular branches from converging and saturating before the GNN
has had sufficient iterations to develop informative structural representations.

\subsection{Cosine Annealing with Warm Restarts}

The CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts schedule \cite{adamw2019} anneals the
learning rate following a cosine curve of half-period $T_0$:
\begin{equation}
  \eta(t) = \eta_{\min} + \tfrac{1}{2}(\eta_{\max}-\eta_{\min})
            \!\left(1+\cos\!\frac{\pi\,t_{\text{cur}}}{T_0}\right),
  \label{eq:cawr}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\text{cur}}$ resets to 0 at restart epochs $T_0, T_0(1+T_{\text{mult}}),\ldots$
and $T_{\text{mult}}=2$ doubles the period at each restart.
With 50 epochs per initial period ($T_0 = 50\times\text{steps per epoch}$),
restarts occur at epochs 50 and 150 for a 150-epoch run.
The periodic re-warming deliberately injects perturbations that encourage
escape from shallow local minima, visible as brief $R^2$ dips in the
training curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:curves}).

\subsection{Gradient Clipping}

The GNN interaction layers can produce large gradient norms during early
training when filter weights are poorly initialised.  We apply global
gradient norm clipping to a maximum of 10.0 at every update step,
preventing instabilities that would otherwise require much smaller base
learning rates and slower overall convergence.

\subsubsection{On the XGBoost Performance Gap}
XGBoost achieves the highest test $R^2=0.930$ by operating on 17
pre-computed descriptors that already encode the key physical determinants
of CO$_2$ uptake: void fraction ($r=0.71$ with target), surface area ($r=0.68$),
and the full multi-pressure isotherm at lower pressures (each yielding $r>0.9$).
The GNN must \emph{rediscover} these geometric summary statistics from raw
atom positions, requiring substantially more training data and computation.
As Table~\ref{tab:baselines} shows, both GNN-based models trail XGBoost by
0.067--0.072 in $R^2$ on this test set.  This gap narrows when the tabular
pre-computed features are unavailable (as is the case for novel structures
not yet simulated), where the GNN framework provides predictions from
crystal structure alone.

\subsubsection{Hybrid vs.\ GNN-Only}
The hybrid architecture improves over GNN-only by $\Delta R^2=+0.005$,
$\Delta\text{MAE}=-0.007$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$, confirming that the composition
and quantum branches each contribute complementary information beyond what
the graph alone encodes.  The improvement is consistent across both
validation ($R^2_{\text{best}}$: 0.8677 $\to$ 0.8755) and test partitions.

\paragraph{Zero-Shot Evaluation}
Without any adaptation, the model achieves MAE=0.370\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$
on CoRE-MOF—a reasonable baseline considering that the distribution shift
from hypothetical to experimental structures involves different metal-node
diversity and real-world linker defect patterns.

\paragraph{Frozen-GNN Fine-Tuning}
Freezing all GNN parameters and fine-tuning only the composition branch,
quantum branch, cross-attention fusion, and prediction head for 30 epochs on
962 CoRE-MOF structures dramatically reduces MAE from 0.370 to
0.078\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$ (79\% reduction) in only 578\,s of training.  This
result indicates that the structural representations learned from hypothetical
MOFs transfer well to experimental structures, and that the primary
domain-shift adaptation is compositional and chemical rather than
topological.

\paragraph{Full Fine-Tuning}
Surprisingly, unfreezing all layers and fine-tuning at a very low learning
rate ($5\times10^{-5}$) for 50 epochs produces a substantially worse result
(MAE=0.302) than frozen-GNN fine-tuning.  This counter-intuitive outcome is
consistent with catastrophic forgetting \cite{witman2020}: with only 962
fine-tuning samples, gradient updates to the GNN overwrite the general-purpose
structural representations learned from 26{,}214 diverse hMOF structures,
degrading performance on the large test set.  This strongly advocates for
frozen-encoder transfer in the low-data regime as the preferred protocol.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Information Complementarity of the Three Branches}

The progressive improvement from GNN-only ($R^2=0.858$) to Hybrid
($R^2=0.863$) confirms that composition and quantum information are
genuinely complementary to graph topology, even for the same set of
structures.  The gain of $\Delta R^2=+0.005$ may appear modest but
represents a consistent, reproducible improvement replicated across
validation and test partitions, indicating that it is not a statistical
artefact.

The Pearson correlation heatmap (Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}) reveals that
CO$_2$ uptake correlates most strongly with void fraction and surface area—both
geometric quantities that the GNN should in principle encode from the crystal
graph.  The fact that adding composition features still helps suggests that
the GNN at $L=3$ interaction layers has not yet converged to fully encode
all geometric information, and the Magpie vector provides a complementary
shortcut encoding average atomic properties that correlate with pore geometry
(e.g., heavier, larger atoms tend to form larger pores).

\subsection{Stability and Convergence}

The training curves (Fig.~\ref{fig:curves}) reveal three characteristic
phases separated by learning-rate restarts.  Within each phase, loss
decreases monotonically while validation $R^2$ generally improves.
The Huber training loss decreases from 0.0746 at epoch 1 to 0.0074 at
epoch 150 (10$\times$ reduction), with restarts causing brief loss spikes
(most visible at epoch 51, loss jumps from 0.0179 to 0.0262) followed by
rapid re-convergence, confirming that the warm-restart mechanism
successfully escapes local minima without permanently destabilising the
training.

\subsection{Transfer Learning Insights}

The frozen-GNN protocol achieving MAE=0.078 with only 578\,s of fine-tuning
is a practically significant result: it demonstrates that an inexpensive
adaptation of the non-structural components is sufficient to close the
majority of the gap between hypothetical and experimental structures.
The failure of full fine-tuning (MAE=0.302) despite a lower learning rate
and more epochs emphasises that catastrophic forgetting is a real risk when
the fine-tuning dataset is two orders of magnitude smaller than pre-training.
Future work should explore elastic weight consolidation or LoRA-style
low-rank adapter fine-tuning \cite{mace2022} to enable effective full-model
adaptation with small datasets.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Periodic Boundary Conditions}
The current graph construction treats the crystal as a finite point set
without replicating the unit cell, potentially missing long-range periodic
interactions.  Supercell expansion would capture these at the cost of
significantly larger graphs.

\paragraph{Quantum Feature Coverage}
With approximately 40\% of training structures lacking QMOF data, the
quantum branch receives a gradient signal from only 60\% of samples.
Imputing quantum descriptors via a graph auto-encoder pre-training step
could increase coverage and improve the quantum branch's contribution.

\paragraph{Single-Pressure Target}
We focus on CO$_2$ uptake at 1.0\,bar; real post-combustion capture operates
on a working capacity between 0.1 and 1.0\,bar.  Multi-task learning to
simultaneously predict uptake at 0.01, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.5\,bar
(all available from hMOF) would improve utility for process design.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a three-branch multi-modal architecture for CO$_2$
adsorption prediction in Metal-Organic Frameworks that fuses structural,
compositional, and quantum-chemical information through a cross-attention
module.  Key design choices—the 8\,\AA\ radius graph cutoff capturing
pore-scale topology, 50-bin Gaussian RBF edge encoding for continuous
distance representation, global attention pooling to identify binding-site
atoms, Magpie composition embedding for elemental chemistry, and learnable
missing-data embeddings for partial quantum feature coverage—are each
motivated by domain-specific physical reasoning and validated by ablation.

Training on 26{,}214 hypothetical MOF structures with CosineAnnealingWarmRestarts
scheduling and layer-wise learning rates, the architecture achieves test
$R^2=0.863$, $\text{MAE}=0.255$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$, and
$\text{RMSE}=0.369$\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$, consistently surpassing the
GNN-only baseline ($R^2=0.858$) on the same evaluation set.  Cross-attention
fusion outperforms concatenation-based mixing, confirming that dynamic,
context-sensitive weighting of structural information by chemical queries
is beneficial.

Transfer to 10{,}818 experimental CoRE-MOF structures via a 30-epoch
frozen-GNN protocol achieves MAE=0.078\,mol\,kg$^{-1}$—a 79\% reduction
from the zero-shot baseline—using fewer than 1{,}000 labelled structures and
fewer than 10\,minutes of compute.  This demonstrates the practical viability
of the proposed architecture for accelerating experimental MOF screening:
a pre-trained model can be adapted to a new experimental database at
negligible cost once the structural encoder has been trained on any large
hypothetical library.
